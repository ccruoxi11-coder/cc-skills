# STARD 2015 — diagnostic accuracy studies (+ QUADAS-2 for risk of bias)

**Source:** Bossuyt PM, Reitsma JB, et al. *STARD 2015: an updated list of essential items for reporting
diagnostic accuracy studies.* BMJ 2015;351:h5527 (and Radiology 2015). A **30-item** checklist + flow
diagram. Risk-of-bias companion: **QUADAS-2** (Whiting et al., Ann Intern Med 2011). Verify items
against the official STARD list.

> Use when the claim is diagnostic/screening accuracy (sensitivity/specificity, PPV/NPV) against a
> reference standard — common for imaging-AI and biosignal screening tools.

## Domains and high-miss items

**Title/Abstract/Keywords**
- Identifies the study as a diagnostic accuracy study; structured abstract.

**Introduction**
- Scientific/clinical background; study objectives & hypotheses.

**Methods**
- **Study design** — prospective vs retrospective; whether data collection was planned before the
  index test and reference standard were performed.
- **Participants** — eligibility, setting, sampling (consecutive/random/convenience), recruitment dates.
- **Index test & reference standard** — how performed, in sufficient detail to replicate; rationale
  for the reference standard.
- **Blinding** — readers of the index test blinded to the reference standard and vice versa.
- **Methods for estimating diagnostic accuracy**; how indeterminate results / missing data were handled.

**Results**
- **Participant flow diagram** (eligible → tested → analyzed, with exclusions).
- Clinical/demographic characteristics; distribution of disease severity.
- **2×2 table / cross-tabulation** of index vs reference; estimates of accuracy **with 95% CIs**;
  handling of indeterminate results; adverse events.

**Discussion**
- Limitations (sources of bias, applicability/generalizability); clinical implications.

**Other**
- Registration; protocol availability; **funding**; data availability.

## QUADAS-2 risk-of-bias domains (for reviews / self-assessment)

Patient selection, index test, reference standard, flow & timing — each rated for risk of bias and
applicability.

## Cross-disciplinary red flags

- No clear reference standard or readers not blinded.
- Spectrum bias (case-control "diseased vs healthy" instead of the intended clinical population).
- Accuracy without CIs; indeterminate results dropped silently; operating point chosen on the test set.
